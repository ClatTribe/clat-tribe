International Law March 29, 2026 • 8 min read +30 XP

ICJ Delivers Landmark Advisory Opinion on Climate Change Obligations of States

The International Court of Justice has taken a historic step, establishing for the first time that states have binding obligations under international law to mitigate greenhouse gas emissions.

In a watershed moment for international environmental law, the International Court of Justice (ICJ) delivered its advisory opinion on the obligations of states with respect to climate change on March 21, 2026. The opinion, requested by the UN General Assembly in a resolution co-sponsored by Vanuatu and 132 other nations, addresses whether existing international legal frameworks impose binding duties on states to reduce greenhouse gas emissions and protect vulnerable nations from climate harm.

Background of the Advisory Opinion

The campaign for an ICJ advisory opinion on climate change began in 2019, spearheaded by Pacific Island law students who recognized that rising sea levels posed an existential threat to their nations. The UN General Assembly formally submitted the request in March 2023, framing two key questions: What are the obligations of states under international law to ensure the protection of the climate system? And what are the legal consequences for states whose actions cause significant harm to the climate system?

Key Holdings of the Court

The ICJ held, by a vote of 12-3, that states have binding obligations under both customary international law and treaty law to take meaningful action to mitigate greenhouse gas emissions. The court identified three primary sources of obligation: the "no harm" principle under customary international law, obligations arising from the UNFCCC and the Paris Agreement, and the duty of due diligence in preventing transboundary environmental harm. Importantly, the Court held that the Paris Agreement's temperature targets of 1.5°C and 2°C above pre-industrial levels constitute binding benchmarks, not merely aspirational goals.

"The obligation to prevent significant harm to the climate system is not a mere aspiration but a binding duty rooted in the foundational principles of international law, including the principle of sovereignty, the duty of due diligence, and intergenerational equity."

— ICJ Advisory Opinion, Para 78

Implications for India

India, as the world's third-largest emitter of greenhouse gases, faces significant implications. While advisory opinions are non-binding, they carry substantial legal weight and can influence the interpretation of existing treaties. India's argument of "common but differentiated responsibilities" (CBDR) was acknowledged by the Court, which held that while all states have obligations, the extent of those obligations must reflect historical emissions, economic capacity, and developmental needs.

CLAT Relevance

This topic is highly relevant for CLAT GK and Legal Reasoning sections. Expect questions on ICJ advisory jurisdiction (Article 65 of the ICJ Statute), the principle of CBDR, the legal status of the Paris Agreement, and the distinction between advisory opinions and contentious cases.

Key Constitutional Amendments

A visual timeline of the most CLAT-relevant amendments.

1st

1st Amendment (1951)

Added Ninth Schedule to protect land reform laws from judicial review. Introduced reasonable restrictions on freedom of speech under Article 19(2).

7th

7th Amendment (1956)

Reorganization of states on linguistic basis. Abolished Part A/B/C/D classification. Created Union Territories category.

24th

24th Amendment (1971)

Affirmed Parliament's power to amend Fundamental Rights. Response to Golaknath case. Made it mandatory for President to give assent to amendment bills.

42nd

42nd Amendment (1976) — "Mini Constitution"

Added 'Socialist', 'Secular', 'Integrity' to Preamble. Added Fundamental Duties (Art. 51A). Curtailed judicial review. Extended Lok Sabha term to 6 years.

44th

44th Amendment (1978)

Reversed many 42nd Amendment changes. Restored Lok Sabha term to 5 years. Right to Property removed from Fundamental Rights (now Art. 300A).

73rd

73rd & 74th Amendments (1992)

Constitutional status to Panchayati Raj (73rd) and Municipalities (74th). Added Part IX and IX-A. Mandated 1/3 reservation for women in local bodies.

101

101st Amendment (2016)

Introduced Goods and Services Tax (GST). Added Article 246A. Created GST Council (Art. 279A). Subsumed 17 indirect taxes.

How a Bill Becomes Law

The legislative process in the Indian Parliament.

1. Introduction (First Reading)

Bill introduced in either House. Leave to introduce is sought.

arrow_downward

2. Committee Stage

Referred to Standing/Select Committee. Clause-by-clause examination.

arrow_downward

3. Second Reading & Discussion

General discussion on principles. Committee reports examined. Amendments moved.

arrow_downward

4. Third Reading & Voting

Bill put to vote. Simple majority for Ordinary Bills. Special majority for Constitutional Amendments.

Legal Knowledge & Judiciary

Indian Judiciary Structure

Supreme Court: CJI + 33 judges (max 34). Original, Appellate & Advisory jurisdiction

High Courts: 25 HCs. Art. 214–231. Writ jurisdiction under Art. 226 (wider than Art. 32)

Collegium: CJI + 4 senior-most judges recommend SC appointments

Retirement: SC judges at 65, HC judges at 62

Landmark Supreme Court Cases

Golaknath (1967): Parliament cannot amend Fundamental Rights

Kesavananda Bharati (1973): Basic Structure Doctrine established

Maneka Gandhi (1978): Art. 21 includes right to travel abroad; expanded due process

Vishaka (1997): Sexual harassment guidelines at workplace

Puttaswamy (2017): Right to Privacy is a fundamental right under Art. 21

Five Constitutional Writs

Habeas Corpus: "Produce the body" — against illegal detention

Mandamus: "We command" — directs a public official to perform duty

Prohibition: Higher court forbids lower court from exceeding jurisdiction

Economy & Finance

RBI & Monetary Policy

Founded: April 1, 1935. Nationalised: 1949. HQ: Mumbai

Governor: Appointed by Central Govt for 3 years (extendable)

MPC: 6 members (3 RBI + 3 external). Decides repo rate; target: 4% CPI ±2%

Tools: Repo, Reverse Repo, CRR (4%), SLR (18%), Open Market Ops

GST Framework

Launched: July 1, 2017 (101st Amendment). Replaced 17 taxes

GST Council: Art. 279A. Union FM (Chair) + all State FMs. Decisions by 3/4 majority

Rate Slabs: 0%, 5%, 12%, 18%, 28% + Cess on luxury & sin goods

Types: CGST + SGST (intra-state), IGST (inter-state)

Union Budget & Fiscal Terms

Art. 112: Annual Financial Statement. Presented on Feb 1

Fiscal Deficit: Total expenditure − Total receipts (excl. borrowings)

Revenue Deficit: Revenue expenditure − Revenue receipts

Money Bill: Art. 110. Only in Lok Sabha. Speaker's certification is final

NITI Aayog & Planning

Replaced: Planning Commission (Jan 1, 2015). Not a constitutional body

Chair: Prime Minister. CEO appointed by PM

Vision: 15-year Vision Document, 7-year Strategy, 3-year Action Agenda

SDG India Index: Ranks states on 16 SDG goals (Kerala, Tamil Nadu top performers)
