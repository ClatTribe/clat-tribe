Going downhill: On the Iran war, U.S. strategy

An analysis of the escalating US-Israel conflict with Iran and its global ramifications.

The US-Israel war against Iran, which commenced on February 28, has now entered its second month with escalating tensions and contradictory messaging from American leadership. President Trump initially suggested the conflict would conclude swiftly, yet subsequently issued threatening statements regarding Iran's power plants and the country's control over the Strait of Hormuz, repeatedly extending diplomatic deadlines. Despite assertions of successfully destroying Iran's military infrastructure, Tehran has demonstrated continued capacity to conduct attacks against US bases stationed throughout the Persian Gulf region and Israeli targets. Notably, a recent Iranian strike damaged a US AWACS surveillance aircraft stationed at Prince Sultan Air Base in Saudi Arabia. Diplomatic prospects remain dim, with Trump claiming talks are underway while Iran maintains that negotiations are impossible without a cessation of bombing campaigns. The conflict has produced severe global consequences: crude oil prices have surged dramatically, maritime shipping routes in the Strait of Hormuz and Red Sea face disruption, and India—as the world's third-largest oil importer—experiences particular vulnerability. European allies have voiced criticism of American strategic decisions, while the war has paradoxically strengthened diplomatic and economic ties between Iran, China, and Russia.

West Asia crisis must spur efficient use of resources, search for alternatives

India's structural dependence on petrochemicals creates acute vulnerability to geopolitical disruptions.

The ongoing US-Israel-Iran conflict is exerting far more comprehensive impact on India's economy compared to the petroleum crises of the 1970s, primarily because India's structural dependence on petrochemicals has expanded exponentially in recent decades. Contemporary Indian society consumes approximately 70 million tonnes of fertilisers annually, maintains over 330 million liquefied petroleum gas (LPG) connections, derives 60 percent of textile production from synthetic fibres, and relies ubiquitously on polymers across multiple economic sectors. The current energy crisis represents India's potential version of 1970s-era stagflation—a combination of stagnant economic growth and persistent inflation. India imports 88 percent of its crude oil requirements and 48 percent of natural gas consumption from global markets. With Brent crude oil trading at $115 per barrel and natural gas prices tripling relative to pre-conflict levels, Indian policymakers must prioritize energy efficiency and the systematic development of alternative energy sources. Strategic recommendations include transitioning fertiliser applications from conventional urea to nano-enhanced variants, accelerating electric vehicle adoption, significantly expanding renewable solar and nuclear energy capacity, reducing structural dependence on polymer-based products, and improving petroleum refinery operational efficiency. Government authorities must demonstrate political courage in resisting populist fuel subsidy schemes and instead allocate investment resources toward comprehensive structural economic transformation and diversification.

Key Constitutional Amendments

A visual timeline of the most CLAT-relevant amendments.

1st

1st Amendment (1951)

Added Ninth Schedule to protect land reform laws from judicial review. Introduced reasonable restrictions on freedom of speech under Article 19(2).

7th

7th Amendment (1956)

Reorganization of states on linguistic basis. Abolished Part A/B/C/D classification. Created Union Territories category.

24th

24th Amendment (1971)

Affirmed Parliament's power to amend Fundamental Rights. Response to Golaknath case. Made it mandatory for President to give assent to amendment bills.

42nd

42nd Amendment (1976) — "Mini Constitution"

Added 'Socialist', 'Secular', 'Integrity' to Preamble. Added Fundamental Duties (Art. 51A). Curtailed judicial review. Extended Lok Sabha term to 6 years.

44th

44th Amendment (1978)

Reversed many 42nd Amendment changes. Restored Lok Sabha term to 5 years. Right to Property removed from Fundamental Rights (now Art. 300A).

73rd

73rd & 74th Amendments (1992)

Constitutional status to Panchayati Raj (73rd) and Municipalities (74th). Added Part IX and IX-A. Mandated 1/3 reservation for women in local bodies.

101

101st Amendment (2016)

Introduced Goods and Services Tax (GST). Added Article 246A. Created GST Council (Art. 279A). Subsumed 17 indirect taxes.

How a Bill Becomes Law

The legislative process in the Indian Parliament.

1. Introduction (First Reading)

Bill introduced in either House. Leave to introduce is sought.

arrow_downward

2. Committee Stage

Referred to Standing/Select Committee. Clause-by-clause examination.

arrow_downward

3. Second Reading & Discussion

General discussion on principles. Committee reports examined. Amendments moved.

arrow_downward

4. Third Reading & Voting

Bill put to vote. Simple majority for Ordinary Bills. Special majority for Constitutional Amendments.

arrow_downward

5. Transmission to Other House

Same process repeats. Deadlock → Joint Sitting (Art. 108) for Ordinary Bills only.

arrow_downward

6. Presidential Assent (Art. 111)

President can assent, withhold, or return (except Money Bills). If returned and re-passed, President must assent.

Indian Polity & Constitution

The Preamble

Keywords: Sovereign, Socialist, Secular, Democratic, Republic

Added by 42nd Amendment (1976): Socialist, Secular, Integrity

Based on: Objectives Resolution by Jawaharlal Nehru (Dec 13, 1946)

Key Case: Kesavananda Bharati (1973) — Preamble is part of Constitution

Fundamental Rights (Art. 12–35)

Art. 14: Equality before law & equal protection of laws

Art. 19: 6 freedoms (speech, assembly, association, movement, residence, profession)

Art. 21: Right to life & personal liberty (widest scope via judicial interpretation)

Art. 32: Right to Constitutional Remedies — "Heart & Soul" of the Constitution (Ambedkar)

Key Constitutional Amendments

1st (1951): Added 9th Schedule; restrictions on free speech

42nd (1976): "Mini-Constitution" — added Socialist, Secular, Integrity; Fundamental Duties

44th (1978): Reversed many 42nd changes; Right to Property no longer fundamental

73rd & 74th (1992): Panchayati Raj & Municipalities given constitutional status

101st (2016): GST introduced; GST Council under Art. 279A

Constitutional Bodies & Offices

President: Elected by Electoral College (Art. 54). Term: 5 years. Impeachment under Art. 61

Vice President: Ex-officio Chairman of Rajya Sabha (Art. 64)

CAG: Art. 148–151. Audits all govt expenditure. Appointed by President

Election Commission: Art. 324. CEC + 2 ECs. CEC removal = same as SC judge

gavel

Legal Knowledge & Judiciary

Indian Judiciary Structure

Supreme Court: CJI + 33 judges (max 34). Original, Appellate & Advisory jurisdiction

High Courts: 25 HCs. Art. 214–231. Writ jurisdiction under Art. 226 (wider than Art. 32)

Collegium: CJI + 4 senior-most judges recommend SC appointments

Retirement: SC judges at 65, HC judges at 62

Landmark Supreme Court Cases

Golaknath (1967): Parliament cannot amend Fundamental Rights

Kesavananda Bharati (1973): Basic Structure Doctrine established

Maneka Gandhi (1978): Art. 21 includes right to travel abroad; expanded due process

Vishaka (1997): Sexual harassment guidelines at workplace

Puttaswamy (2017): Right to Privacy is a fundamental right under Art. 21

Five Constitutional Writs

Habeas Corpus: "Produce the body" — against illegal detention

Mandamus: "We command" — directs a public official to perform duty

Prohibition: Higher court forbids lower court from exceeding jurisdiction

Certiorari: Higher court quashes order of lower court

Quo Warranto: "By what authority?" — challenges person holding public office

New Criminal Laws (2023)

IPC → BNS: Bharatiya Nyaya Sanhita (356 sections replacing 511)

CrPC → BNSS: Bharatiya Nagarik Suraksha Sanhita

Evidence Act → BSA: Bharatiya Sakshya Adhiniyam

Key change: Sedition (S.124A IPC) replaced by S.152 BNS (narrower scope)

Economy & Finance

RBI & Monetary Policy

Founded: April 1, 1935. Nationalised: 1949. HQ: Mumbai

Governor: Appointed by Central Govt for 3 years (extendable)

MPC: 6 members (3 RBI + 3 external). Decides repo rate; target: 4% CPI ±2%

Tools: Repo, Reverse Repo, CRR (4%), SLR (18%), Open Market Ops

GST Framework

Launched: July 1, 2017 (101st Amendment). Replaced 17 taxes

GST Council: Art. 279A. Union FM (Chair) + all State FMs. Decisions by 3/4 majority

Rate Slabs: 0%, 5%, 12%, 18%, 28% + Cess on luxury & sin goods

Types: CGST + SGST (intra-state), IGST (inter-state)

Union Budget & Fiscal Terms

Art. 112: Annual Financial Statement. Presented on Feb 1

Fiscal Deficit: Total expenditure − Total receipts (excl. borrowings)

Revenue Deficit: Revenue expenditure − Revenue receipts

Money Bill: Art. 110. Only in Lok Sabha. Speaker's certification is final

NITI Aayog & Planning

Replaced: Planning Commission (Jan 1, 2015). Not a constitutional body

Chair: Prime Minister. CEO appointed by PM

Vision: 15-year Vision Document, 7-year Strategy, 3-year Action Agenda

SDG India Index: Ranks states on 16 SDG goals (Kerala, Tamil Nadu top performers)
